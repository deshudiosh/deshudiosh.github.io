adjective

  2. having the shape of a sphere or ball

Generative constelations of planets, a combination of digital and Śn-digital art.
Created in collaboration with abstract painter Tom Blameworth

I took care of the code, Tom used paint. We share the idea.

Each variant consists of at least one planet.
Every planet is textured with one of over 60 photograped painted "orbs".
Little moons, and stars are also bitmap-based, rest of the picture is generated with code.

Part 2 - Digital art

Generative - what does it mean?

At the root of algorythm constructing each picture, there is a single number called seed. That seed is used in all randomization functions throuout the project.

Everything - which images to use by each planet; light direction; number of orbs and their position and size; presense or lack of cities; moons; planetry rings - is derived from that seed. Every seed yields different Orbicular variant, and one particular seed always yields the same output.

Light

One of the main randomized properties is light direction. It affects nearly every aspect of the image:

  • which side of the planet is lit
  • shadow position under the orb
  • if city is at daytime or nighttime
  • which side of the buildings should be brighter
  • gradient on the background

Elements

Every planet has a chance to spawn each of those fully generative elements:

City

Each city is unique. Randomization begins with very basic characteristics like width and height, or number of buildings, to more complex like rotation, presense of windows, dots that make it blend near the ground level. City colors match the planet, and also the lightining.

Planetary ring

Many semi-transparent white ellipses combined together construct every planetary ring. Varying outter radius, inner radius and rotation attributes control the look. Ellipses thickness is randomized to break the ring's otherwise uniform look.

Color sampling

I developped a custom color sampling module for this project, along with previewer.
The procedure is to get a slighly blurred image from the planet, and set the color at the top edge as a primary (marked with a big red dot). Then create a matrix of sample points (little red dots), and find the secondary color, which will be visually as distant from primary as possible. Distant in terms of hue-saturation-lightness color space. Hue difference is the most important part of the equation.

Background

Background is simple: gradient corresponds with light direction; colors are darkened swatches extracted from biggest orb; stars are randomly scattered little grayscale images. There is also a high chance of falling star appearing.

Orb spawning

Very technical solution, to visually very influential aspect is planet positioning. There are no predetermined compositions in Orbicular. Planets are spawned in a sequence, ensuring there aren't any unwanted collisions, and also fitting the margin.

Collision detection system is based on circles. Rules are:

  • Red circles can't collide with anything (except for circles of the same planet).
  • Green and orange circles behave the same as red, but are allowed to go past blue margin.

Falling star (orange) collision detection serves two purposes. First, it makes it occupy free space in the composition. Second, it prevents "streaks" from ever starting behind any other objects. Small middle-circle allows for close contact though.
